**Abstract:** Foundation models and generative artificial intelligence (AI) are changing how molecules are predicted, searched, and designed across chemistry, biology, and materials science. However, molecular discovery remains limited by a basic mismatch between large search spaces and scarce experimental data. Chemical space is often estimated to contain more than 10^60 possible candidates, while measured labels for a specific property are usually limited to only a few hundred or a few thousand examples. Scientific applications also rarely depend on a single objective. Domain experts need candidates that satisfy multiple and sometimes competing goals, such as high potency and low toxicity in drug discovery, or high permeability and high selectivity in gas separation membranes. These challenges call for molecular AI systems that can learn from limited data, explain their predictions, generate candidates under multiple constraints, connect designs with synthesis routes, and support practical validation. This dissertation develops data-centric learning methods and molecular foundation models for molecular discovery under scarce and imbalanced labels, multimodal observations, and multi-objective design goals. Technically, the developed methods improve the accuracy and interpretability of prediction models for virtual screening, as well as the controllability of generative models for inverse design, including models that generate both molecular structures and possible synthesis routes. The first part introduces data-centric graph learning for molecular property prediction, using subgraph rationales, rationale-guided augmentation, pseudo-labeling, label-anchored mixup, and multimodal alignment to improve prediction under limited, imbalanced, and multimodal supervision. The second part develops Graph Diffusion Transformers as the foundation models for inverse design, supporting molecular generation under categorical and numerical property constraints, integration with large language models for interleaved text, molecular graph, and reaction generation, and in-context molecular design from tens of molecule-property demonstrations. The third part translates these methods into scientific and community impact, including experimentally validated polymer membranes, the open-source package torch-molecule, and the Open Polymer Challenge, which provides a benchmark for polymer property prediction and studies practical issues in data quality, simulation consistency, distribution shift, and model evaluation. Together, these contributions connect data, models, synthesis planning, software, benchmarks, and experiments into a molecular AI workflow for prediction, design, and validation. The dissertation concludes by outlining future directions toward new molecular foundation models, open infrastructure for collaborative molecular AI, and agentic AI scientists that can support closed-loop discovery across molecules and scientific systems.

**Abstract:** Molecular docking is widely used in structure-based drug discovery, yet most approaches provide point estimates without rigorous uncertainty quantification. This limitation makes it difficult to assess when a predicted pose should be trusted, especially when docking methods are applied to diverse protein-ligand systems. We present ConfDock, a conformal prediction (CP) framework for constructing atom-specific prediction intervals for ligand docking poses. ConfDock combines graph neural network (GNN) based quantile estimation with split conformal calibration, producing intervals that adapt to local protein-ligand environments while retaining distribution-free finite-sample coverage guarantees. We evaluate ConfDock on 238 protein-ligand complexes across four docking methods representing distinct computational paradigms. The proposed approach yields substantially narrower prediction intervals compared to standard split CP (57.2% average reduction in mean interval width, up to 74.5%) while maintaining target coverage across all evaluated settings. Ablation analysis indicates that the GNN captures the dominant structure-dependent variability in uncertainty, whereas the conformal calibration step provides a bounded adjustment to ensure coverage guarantees. These results demonstrate that combining learned, structure-aware quantile estimation with conformal calibration enables rigorous uncertainty quantification for molecular docking at atom-level resolution.
