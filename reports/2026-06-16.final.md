## 1. MoE-Bind: Guiding De Novo Protein Binder Generation with Sparse Experts

Link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/13//2026.06.13.732043.source.xml  
Source: biorxiv | Date: 2026-06-13 | Tags: generative_modeling_core, peptide_design, protein_design, protein_ligand_affinity

**Motivation:** Structure-based binder design pipelines can be slow and expensive because they depend on target structures and repeated structural inference. MoE-Bind attacks the bottleneck by asking whether sequence-only generation can gain capacity and specialization without paying dense-transformer compute costs.

**Methods:** The model is an autoregressive receptor-conditioned protein binder generator using Multi-head Latent Attention plus sparse Mixture-of-Experts feed-forward layers. Sparse routing activates only a subset of parameters per token, and the authors analyze expert assignments to connect routing behavior with amino-acid and biochemical patterns.

**Experiments:** Evaluation uses a leakage-free Docking Benchmark 5.0 setup and two independent structure predictors, Boltz-2 and AlphaFold2-Multimer. The model is compared with compute-matched dense baselines on full-length binder generation and is also tested for transfer to short-peptide design.

**Innovation:** The main contribution is importing sparse expert architectures into sequence-only protein binder generation while preserving or improving design metrics. The routing analysis is also valuable because it suggests a path from black-box generation toward interpretable expert-level design control.

**Potential Weaknesses:** The strongest validation still flows through structure predictors rather than large-scale wet-lab binding data. Expert specialization may be sensitive to training data composition, and the abstract does not establish how well routing behavior can be deliberately controlled.

**Insights:** Sparse capacity looks especially relevant for biological sequence generation, where local biochemical regimes may benefit from specialized subnetworks. This is the most important paper in the packet because the method is both computationally practical and transferable to other conditioned biological generation tasks.

## 2. ProtAff: Protein Binding Affinity Prediction via LoRA-Finetuned ESM-2

Link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/13//2026.06.13.732058.source.xml  
Source: biorxiv | Date: 2026-06-13 | Tags: protein_design, protein_ligand_affinity

**Motivation:** Current structure predictors can often produce plausible complex geometries, but their confidence metrics do not reliably rank affinity among known binders. ProtAff targets the practical design problem of within-target ranking when many candidate binders must be prioritized.

**Methods:** ProtAff fine-tunes ESM-2 with LoRA and adds a cross-attention module for protein-pair affinity prediction. It trains with a margin ranking loss on 362,567 affinity measurements from 20 data sources, with explicit sequence-similarity filtering for the EGFR test target.

**Experiments:** On the AdaptyvBio EGFR benchmark, ProtAff reaches Spearman rho = 0.413, ahead of AF3 and Boltz-2 confidence metrics and ahead of MINT and CrossAffinity. In a Nipah binder design competition workflow, ProtAff-based ranking yielded a 0.132 nM binder, while cross-target discrimination remained weaker than structural methods.

**Innovation:** The paper makes a clear distinction between affinity ranking and structural confidence, then optimizes directly for ranking. Its value is not only the LoRA recipe but also the evidence that sequence models can complement structure models in the ranking stage of binder design.

**Potential Weaknesses:** The method underperforms for cross-target specificity, so it should not be treated as a universal interaction classifier. Reported gains may depend heavily on data curation and target similarity controls.

**Insights:** A strong binder-design stack may separate generation, structure plausibility, and affinity ranking into distinct models. ProtAff is a useful reminder that high-quality structures and high-affinity binders are related but not interchangeable objectives.

## 3. Reinforcement learning-driven unified generative framework for multi-objective RNA codon design

Link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/13//2026.06.12.732012.source.xml  
Source: biorxiv | Date: 2026-06-13 | Tags: multi_objective_optimization, ai_drug_discovery, transferable_insights

**Motivation:** RNA codon design often separates sequence generation from downstream optimization, which can make long-sequence design inefficient and brittle across organisms or RNA classes. RNARL aims to make generation itself the optimization process.

**Methods:** The framework uses reinforcement learning to generate codon sequences while directly optimizing multiple objectives. Rather than producing candidates and then running a detached optimizer, the policy learns to emit high-performing sequences under the design criteria.

**Experiments:** The abstract reports optimization over sequences longer than 3,900 nucleotides and tests across six species and five RNA types. The authors claim improved performance and broad generalization relative to existing codon-design approaches.

**Innovation:** The unified generate-and-optimize formulation is the key idea. For RNA therapeutics, the ability to handle long sequences and multiple organism/RNA contexts in one learning framework is more interesting than another narrow codon-usage heuristic.

**Potential Weaknesses:** The abstract gives limited detail about reward design, constraints, and how conflicting objectives are balanced. Real therapeutic utility will depend on whether the optimized objectives predict expression, stability, immunogenicity, and manufacturability in the intended setting.

**Insights:** Reinforcement learning remains useful when design constraints are long-range, discrete, and multi-objective. RNARL may also be a pattern for other biological sequence tasks where post-hoc optimization repeatedly breaks generator assumptions.

## 4. Unlocking Your Programmable and Creative RNA Sequence Designer with RDiffusion

Link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/13//2026.06.13.732023.source.xml  
Source: biorxiv | Date: 2026-06-13 | Tags: generative_modeling_core, ai_drug_discovery

**Motivation:** RNA design spans diverse constraints such as function, family, secondary or tertiary structure, and protein binding. RDiffusion attempts to provide a programmable RNA generator that can condition on multiple biological specifications rather than solving each design task separately.

**Methods:** RDiffusion is a diffusion-based RNA sequence model conditioned on biological features including desired function, family type, structural information, and binding proteins. The model is framed both as a design system and as an RNA representation model for downstream tasks.

**Experiments:** The authors evaluate across a broad RNA design suite, reporting better design success and sequence diversity than baselines and state-of-the-art downstream performance. They also describe an osteoarthritis miRNA design case driven by seed selection and screening, with biological validation still in progress.

**Innovation:** The attractive feature is conditional programmability across RNA design axes. A single diffusion framework that can absorb functional and structural constraints could become a reusable layer for RNA engineering workflows.

**Potential Weaknesses:** The disease-design section has not yet completed biological validation. Diffusion performance may be task-dependent, and the abstract does not reveal how the method handles hard biological constraints during sampling.

**Insights:** RNA design is moving toward multi-condition generative models rather than task-specific generators. The paper is worth tracking because conditional RNA diffusion could become a general interface between biological annotations and sequence generation.

## 5. Circuit Tracing in Autoregressive Protein Language Models

Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.16044v1  
Source: arxiv | Date: 2026-06-14 | Tags: discrete_generative_modeling, protein_design

**Motivation:** Protein language models can generate useful sequences, but designers still have little visibility into the computations that drive generation and fitness scoring. This paper targets interpretability for autoregressive protein generation rather than only representation learning.

**Methods:** ProGenMech extends cross-layer transcoders to ProGen3, a sparse MoE protein model trained for causal generation and span infilling. Cross-layer latent variables reconstruct each layer from preceding layers, enabling circuit discovery across the generative computation.

**Experiments:** The framework is tested on causal generation, zero-shot fitness estimation, and span infilling. It outperforms local transcoder baselines in recovering probability distributions and scoring behavior while preserving the model's span-infilling distribution.

**Innovation:** The work adapts mechanistic interpretability to the autoregressive protein setting and links circuits to motifs, functional regions, and fitness landscapes. That makes it more actionable for model steering than generic post-hoc attribution.

**Potential Weaknesses:** Circuit recovery fidelity does not automatically imply biological causality. The approach is tied to a specific model family and may require substantial engineering to transfer to other protein generators.

**Insights:** Interpretability for design models should explain generative decisions, not just static embeddings. Cross-layer circuit tools could eventually support constrained editing, motif-level steering, and failure analysis in protein sequence generators.

## 6. Ubiquitin Receptor-Induced Proximity is Sufficient for Ubiquitin-Independent Targeted Protein Degradation via the 26S Proteasome

Link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/13/2025.08.18.670774.source.xml  
Source: biorxiv | Date: 2026-06-13 | Tags: molecular_glue_and_tpd

**Motivation:** Most targeted protein degradation strategies route targets through ubiquitination machinery, which can limit target scope and introduce dependence on E3 ligase biology. This paper asks whether direct proteasome recruitment can be sufficient for selective degradation.

**Methods:** The authors introduce Protea-Tac, a heterobifunctional protein degrader built from a ubiquitin receptor and an intracellular antibody. The construct recruits selected proteins directly to engineered 26S proteasomes while bypassing ubiquitination.

**Experiments:** Protea-Tac is tested against targets including c-Fos, BRD4, Flag-TDP43, HA-tau, and GFP-ODC. The platform is reported to be modular, ubiquitin-independent, target-specific, compatible with proteasome function, and active in an in vivo anti-tumor setting through c-Fos degradation.

**Innovation:** The mechanism is distinct from standard PROTAC or molecular glue logic: it uses proteasome-proximity rather than E3-mediated ubiquitination. The modular intracellular-antibody design could open new routes for targets that lack good E3 recruitment options.

**Potential Weaknesses:** Intracellular antibody delivery and engineered proteasome deployment are major translational hurdles. The approach may be powerful experimentally before it is drug-like in the conventional small-molecule sense.

**Insights:** The conceptual result is valuable even if the exact construct is not immediately therapeutic: proximity to the proteasome can be treated as a design axis independent of ubiquitin tagging.

## 7. MA-SBI: Misspecification-Aware Simulation-Based Inference via Side-Channel Guidance

Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.16923v1  
Source: arxiv | Date: 2026-06-15 | Tags: flow_matching_and_diffusion

**Motivation:** Simulation-based inference often fails when the simulator differs from reality, and calibration data with true parameters is rarely available. MA-SBI exploits side information such as labels, policy text, or regime descriptions as a practical signal for correcting posterior bias.

**Methods:** The method learns a corrector that maps side-channel text to an observation-space shift before applying a pretrained amortized posterior. It avoids posterior retraining and does not require ground-truth calibration pairs; the theory bounds bias reduction through mutual information between misspecification and side-channel information.

**Experiments:** On hide-the-calibration benchmarks, text-guided MA-SBI matches oracle posterior behavior across seeds and backbones. A stochastic version improves posterior-predictive likelihood on COVID and OxCGRT epidemiological data and leaves a well-specified cognitive-science corpus unchanged.

**Innovation:** The key contribution is using unstructured side-channel guidance as a calibration-free correction mechanism for misspecified simulators. That is a broadly transferable idea for scientific ML systems where metadata contains information about regime shifts.

**Potential Weaknesses:** Performance depends on whether side-channel information actually encodes the misspecification. The correction happens in observation space, so severe structural mismatch may still require simulator revision or paired calibration data.

**Insights:** Scientific inference pipelines should treat metadata and regime annotations as first-class statistical objects. MA-SBI offers a compact recipe for combining pretrained amortized inference with lightweight misspecification correction.

## 8. Functional Gradient Descent with Adaptive Representations

Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.16926v1  
Source: arxiv | Date: 2026-06-15

**Motivation:** Functional gradient descent has cleaner optimization theory than parameter optimization in fixed neural representations, but infinite-dimensional gradients make it hard to implement. This work tries to make functional optimization practical without discarding convergence guarantees.

**Methods:** The paper proposes an implementable FGD algorithm that adapts the representation used to approximate functional gradients during optimization. Its analysis explicitly accounts for approximation error and proves convergence to stationary points, and to global minima under a smoothness plus Polyak-Lojasiewicz condition.

**Experiments:** The method is evaluated on regression, PDE solving, and computer vision tasks. Across these settings it outperforms fixed-approximation FGD and neural-network baselines in efficiency and accuracy.

**Innovation:** The adaptive representation is the main methodological advance. Instead of pretending finite approximations are exact, the algorithm makes representation adaptation part of both the procedure and the proof.

**Potential Weaknesses:** The abstract does not specify computational overhead or sensitivity to representation-growth choices. Practical adoption will depend on whether the adaptive machinery remains simple enough for large-scale models.

**Insights:** This paper is worth reading for optimization ideas that may transfer beyond its benchmark tasks. Adaptive functional representations could be useful wherever fixed parameterizations force awkward nonconvex training dynamics.

## 9. PertDiffBench: Benchmarking Diffusion Models for Single-Cell Perturbation Response Prediction

Link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/13//2026.06.13.732013.source.xml  
Source: biorxiv | Date: 2026-06-13

**Motivation:** Diffusion models are increasingly used for perturbation response prediction, but their advantages over simpler baselines are often confounded by datasets, encoders, metrics, and evaluation splits. PertDiffBench creates a more disciplined testbed for this question.

**Methods:** The benchmark spans single-cell and bulk RNA-seq perturbation prediction with three evaluation families: standard known-context prediction, generalization to unseen cell types/species/drugs/time points, and stress tests over feature dimensionality, representation, noise type, and gene ordering.

**Experiments:** The study finds no consistent diffusion advantage. scGen remains strong in common prediction settings, scDiffusion is competitive in some generalization cases, a simple DDPM performs well for temporal imputation, and pretrained encoders do not reliably improve results.

**Innovation:** The contribution is a benchmark that separates model architecture from representation and evaluation artifacts. The negative result is useful because it challenges the assumption that diffusion is automatically better for transcriptomic perturbation modeling.

**Potential Weaknesses:** Benchmark conclusions will depend on selected datasets, preprocessing, and implementation quality. Newer diffusion variants or task-specific conditioning strategies may change the picture.

**Insights:** For biological prediction tasks, benchmark design may matter more than model family branding. PertDiffBench should help prevent overclaiming when simpler generative or representation-based methods are competitive.

## 10. Polynomial-Time Riesz-Energy Subset Selection for Ordered Point Sets on Lines and l1-Staircases

Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.16946v1  
Source: arxiv | Date: 2026-06-15 | Tags: multi_objective_optimization

**Motivation:** Selecting diverse fixed-size subsets appears in Pareto-front approximation, skyline summarization, and representative design. This paper studies a Riesz-energy formulation where pairwise spacing defines the subset quality.

**Methods:** For ordered one-dimensional points, the authors prove a Monge property for the Riesz interaction. This yields submodularity on a finite distributive lattice and enables polynomial-time optimization, including an explicit minimum S-T cut construction.

**Experiments:** The abstract emphasizes structural results and an accompanying open-source Python implementation. It also extends the construction by isometry to l1 staircases relevant to two-dimensional Pareto or skyline approximations.

**Innovation:** The main value is an exact polynomial-time algorithm for a subset-selection objective that could otherwise be treated with heuristics. The Monge/submodular/min-cut path is clean and likely transferable to related ordered selection problems.

**Potential Weaknesses:** The result is specialized to ordered points on lines and l1 staircases, and bit-complexity depends on arithmetic assumptions. It may not cover the high-dimensional diversity objectives used in many design pipelines.

**Insights:** When a design-selection problem has hidden order structure, exact combinatorial algorithms may beat generic evolutionary or greedy methods. This could be useful for compact Pareto-front selection in multi-objective molecular or materials workflows.
